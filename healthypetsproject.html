HealthyPets: Pet Health Manager App


Redesigning the iOS Shortcuts app to eliminate design inconsistencies and reduce friction in the user's workflow. Aimed to provide more user clarity and lower the learning curve for beginner users.

UX Designer

Grad School Capstone

8 months (2024)

Qualtrics, Microsoft Teams, Figjam, Figma, Justinmind

Project Overview


Context

Final project for our HCID 570 class, which explored design patterns & contexts normally found in everyday world. My team chose to revamp the iOS Shortcuts app, citing its unintuitive user experience and lack of in-app support as potential problems to address.

Brief

Simplify the process of creating shortcuts, shorten the learning curve, and improve usability.

Design Approach

Target Users

Pet owners

Research Methods

  • Literature Review
  • Competitive Analysis
  • User Surveys
  • User Interviews

Design Process

Literature Review

To kickstart research for this project, I conducted an in-depth literature review. My questions to answer were:

  • What services constitute pet healthcare?
  • How are pet owners shopping for pet healthcare services?
  • What are the types of service providers that offer these services?
  • Are there any existing ideas for pet healthcare solutions?
  • How do people remember future appointments?

Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitive landscape analysis for pet healthcare tracking solutions by reviewing the top five pet tracker apps on the market and comparing their features side by side.

Key findings from this analysis shed light on the competitors' strengths, limitations, and opportunities for other features:

  • Strengths: custom appointment reminders, profile management system, and in-app vet interaction support
  • Limitations: automated appointment syncing, data sharing capabilities
  • Other opportunities: pet diary, service provider locator, pet contacts, other helpful resources

User Surveys

The survey was created and distributed on Qualtrics, and aimed to answer three questions:

  • What does the pet healthcare routine look like from the pet owner’s perspective?
  • How are pet owners managing their pet’s service appointment dates?
  • What are their preferences when it comes to a tracking solution?

User Interviews

The user interviews aimed to answer the following question:
What are the challenges and pain points present in the user’s pet care routine?

Methodology

  • Remote session: Interview sessions were conducted over Microsoft Teams
  • Guided interview: Participants answered a list of prepared questions with thorough follow-up queries

After interviews concluded, I applied inductive coding to the transcripts then organized these codes into 3 categories: needs/motivations, behaviors, and frustrations.

Key Findings

01

Pet owners do their best to gauge their pet’s health through observation, but it’s not enough.

02

Pet owners mostly rely on spontaneous thoughts to remember to schedule upcoming services.

03

Pet owners sacrifice their own convenience to get affordable, quality, and timely pet care.

04

Pet owners look for online advice on their pet’s general care or symptoms, but find it confusing, inconsistent, and lacking credibility.

Persona & User Journey

Design Recommendations

After synthesizing all findings from research, I came up with 4 design recommendations for a potential solution:

01

Provide a systematic method for pet owners (and vets) to store and organize pet information: medical records, photos, videos, and relevant notes.

02

Help owners remember and properly prepare for pet healthcare appointments in a more reliable, less spontaneous way.

03

Provide personalized care provider suggestions that fit pet owner’s specific requirements.

04

Provide official resources that offer guidance on general pet care and other information like pet insurance, detecting health problems, and more.

Ideation

From Learnings to Solution Requirements

Crazy Eights

For ideation, I conducted two rounds of Crazy Eights and came up with 14 possible solutions. I also consulted the help of Claude AI to generate more ideas, finally reaching critical mass of 45 total.

Validation

To identify the most effective solution from various design recommendations developed during research, I applied a set of idea selection criteria and a Thinking Hats exercise to these ideas. This helped me cluster key insights and prioritize functionality based on user needs while also eliminating unfeasible ideas.

Because my survey finding indicated a strong preference for a digital-only solution, I opted to create a multipurpose app. I ended up with three feature ideas that were viable within the time and scope constraints of the project:

  • A multi-pet household management system with individual pet profiles and a shareable calendar
  • Weekly health survey with simple Yes/No questions about pet’s changes in habit
  • Emergency contact management

From the ideas, I mapped out required screens. I also mapped out some core workflows to envision the way the user would navigate through the solution.

User Testing #1

The goal of the study was to evaluate the clarity, ease of use, and perceived value of the five aforementioned user flows as well as the app’s core functionality. Key takeaways from user feedback were as follows:

Hi-fi Prototypes

Home > Pet Overview Workflow

  • The top navigation bar was simplified to only include profile settings (which included settings for changing the health survey frequency), while the bottom bar displayed all the available app functions.
  • For the Pet Overview screen, the “Health scores” section was renamed to “Health summary”
  • A secondary CTA was added in the Health summary section to view past summaries

Updating Pet's Health Summary Workflow

For the health survey workflow, I upscaled the design, which included a lock screen nudge that takes you to the survey questions. I added a progress bar at the top so users know exactly where they’re at in the process.

Calendar Screen Functionalities

For the Calendar screen:

  • I added options to allow sharing specific events and dates, not just the entire calendar
  • I also added a completion CTA (and a secondary CTA to repeat the reminder later) to the event detail screen; this is more specific to medical events like giving medicine
  • Users can also export this calendar to their personal calendar, and filter calendar events by the pet, or by the event type
  • I also included a day-hourly view, which gives a more detailed look at any given date

Resources > Contacts Workflow

For the Contacts screen, I added the ability to favorite and pin certain contacts to the top of the list

User Testing #2

This time, I wanted to evaluate the effectiveness of the design fixes, microcopy, and overall visual design of the app.

Final Refinements

Taking into account feedback from the second round of user testing, I added more deliberate color coding to signify a pet’s overall health from the Home screen. In the Pet Overview screen, the Photos & Videos section is now renamed Health archive, and its CTA has been changed to “Add” to allow users to add media or other related files. The last 2 screenshots are from the newly added Past summaries view and Medicine refill view.

Takeaways

Overall, users saw added value in the solution’s core functionalities. They especially liked the routine health surveys since it covered a consistent set of data points that can be monitored over long periods to assess the pet’s overall health. Users also noted the benefits of the Health Archive functionality, saying that it gave them a dedicated place to store information related to their pet’s symptoms and provided a good point of reference for future problems. Other helpful use cases for the this functionality included storing lab reports and pet insurance cards. In regard to the user experience, the intuitiveness of the navigation was praised. Users also felt that all the health information was easy to access and understand.

As for what could be better, users felt that the connection between the pet’s calendar and the owner’s personal calendar could be automated to avoid repeated manual exports. This is a potentially easy fix that I anticipate can be done with some API connections. When it comes to updating the routine health summaries, users thought that it could be beneficial to incorporate readings from smart food/water dispensers to partly automate the process and reduce repetitiveness. I also believe this could quickly be done with API connections.

Future features to be included are:

  • Automatic notifications for scheduling vet visits
  • Dynamic health surveys depending on the pet’s species
  • Event-aware custom reminders
  • Generated care sheets from a selected time frame
  • Vet-verified knowledge base
